## Epic Summary

Epic 2 delivered the interactive landing experience for the refreshed portfolio:

- Card grid layout with 3x2 desktop, 2-column tablet, single-column mobile responsive behavior.
- Dark theme foundation with `bg-base` (#0a0a0a) page surface and gradient card surfaces.
- 5-layer hover effects on interactive cards (cursor-tracking radial glow, scale, gold corner bleed, GoldPulseText pulse, deepened shadow).
- Card expansion overlay system with `React.lazy()` code splitting, `DimmedBackdrop` with backdrop blur, modal semantics, and three close triggers.
- Per-card spring physics animations with unique expansion characters (slide-up, center-scale, bottom-unfold) and content stagger choreography.
- CSS-only ambient background motion with 45s gradient drift cycle and reduced-motion fallback.

All five Epic 2 story files are marked `done` in `sprint-status.yaml`. 38 review findings were generated across 5 stories; all valid findings were fixed before close. 14 items were deferred to `deferred-work.md`.

## What Went Well

- **100% story delivery with zero descoped work.** Every FR assigned to Epic 2 (FR1-FR7, FR35) was satisfied.
- **Sequential story build-up with strong scope discipline.** Each story built cleanly on the last. Explicit "do not pull Story X.Y scope" guardrails appeared in every story and were respected across the entire epic.
- **Thorough review process.** 38 total review findings across 5 stories. Every valid finding was fixed; invalid findings were dismissed with runtime evidence, not guesses. Deferred items were tracked honestly with clear reasoning.
- **Shared component extraction at the right time.** `CardPreview` emerged in 2.1, the lazy-loading registry in 2.3, `ExpandableItem` and `OverlayContentGroup` in 2.4. Each was extracted when reuse became obvious, not over-engineered upfront.
- **Animation boundary pattern consistently respected.** The CSS/Tailwind-owns-hover, Framer-Motion-owns-expansion rule was documented in architecture.md and followed across all 5 stories with zero violations.
- **Story preparation quality was the strongest yet.** Each story context package included repo-grounded guardrails, current file contents, previous story intelligence, and official documentation references.

## Challenges

- **Stale project-context.md carried as friction through all 5 stories.** The Epic 1 retro committed to updating it, but it was never addressed. Every Epic 2 story opened with the same guardrail: "project-context.md still contains stale styled-components guidance, trust the repo instead."
- **Epic 1 action item follow-through was poor.** Only 2 of 5 Epic 1 action items were confirmed done. The live contact/integration verification and CI confirmation remain unaddressed. The project-context update was explicitly not done.
- **Planning doc vs repo truth about card interactivity.** Planning docs say "Cards 2-5 are interactive" while the repo keeps cards 1 and 2 non-interactive. This was repeated as a guardrail in 4 out of 5 stories.
- **Deferred items accumulating without resolution.** Epic 2 added 14 deferred items; zero have been resolved. There is no triage cycle between epics to promote or resolve deferred work.
- **Dev server port reuse created verification ambiguity.** Stories 2.1, 2.2, and 2.5 all encountered `EADDRINUSE` on port 3000 and had to work around an already-running server instead of restarting clean.
- **Hero image visibility during card expansion.** The initial Story 2.3 implementation covered the hero image with a full-viewport overlay. The acceptance criteria explicitly required the hero to remain visible on tablet/desktop, but the deviation was not caught by the developer or the automated review layers — it required Warrick's intervention.
- **Per-card expansion animations lack "wow factor."** The spring configs and motion ranges are tuned conservatively. The distinct character between cards is present but not immediately striking. This may improve when real content fills the overlays in later epics.
- **Ambient background animation is barely perceptible.** The static gradient is visible, but the 45s drift animation at 2-5% gradient opacity is effectively invisible even when looking for it. The implementation meets the letter of the AC but not the spirit of "the dark theme feels alive."

## Key Decisions Made

- **Deferred-item triage becomes a standing epic kickoff ceremony.** Every new epic begins with a review of all accumulated deferred items. Each item is triaged into: incorporate into existing story, dedicated final hardening story, or explicitly deferred to a named future story. Goal: zero orphaned deferred items at epic close.
- **Retro action items require stories, owners, and verification gates.** No more free-floating action items. Every retro commitment gets embedded in a story with AC and a named owner.
- **Clean dev server restart for every verification.** Kill and restart the dev server before verification in every story. No reusing a running server with unclear state.
- **Per-card animation tuning deferred to after Epic 4.** Animations will be reassessed once real content fills the overlay. If they still feel flat, spring configs and motion ranges will be tuned.
- **Ambient background tuning addressed in Epic 3 cycle.** Either incorporated into an existing story or a dedicated tuning task during Epic 3.
- **Hero-adjacent overlay positioning is the accepted product direction.** Tablet and desktop overlays expand into the non-hero grid region so the hero image remains visible behind the dimmed backdrop.

## Takeaways

4. Stale documentation compounds friction across every subsequent story — fix it once, benefit forever.
5. Complex stories increase the risk of acceptance-criteria items being missed, especially when the "easy default" deviates from the specific requirement.
